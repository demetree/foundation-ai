# Counterpoint in the Late Baroque

Counterpoint is the art of combining two or more independent melodic lines in such a way that each retains its own shape while contributing to a coherent harmonic whole. It reaches its high-water mark in the late baroque period, roughly 1685 to 1750, in the work of Johann Sebastian Bach, George Frideric Handel, and a handful of less famous but equally accomplished German organists.

## Species counterpoint

The pedagogical foundation that underlies almost all baroque contrapuntal practice was codified by Johann Joseph Fux in his 1725 treatise *Gradus ad Parnassum*, which presented a graded series of exercises in increasing rhythmic complexity against a fixed melody called the cantus firmus. The five species are: note against note (each voice the same rhythm), two notes against one, four notes against one, syncopated, and finally florid (any combination). Generations of composers including Mozart, Beethoven, and Brahms learned counterpoint by working through this same set of exercises -- it remains in active use in conservatories today.

## Fugue

The fugue is the largest and most demanding contrapuntal form. A fugue begins with a single voice stating a short melodic idea called the subject; subsequent voices enter one by one, each restating the subject -- usually at a different pitch level -- while the previously entered voices continue with new material derived from or in counterpoint to the subject. After all voices have entered (the exposition), the fugue continues through episodes that develop the subject material and middle entries that restate it in various keys, eventually returning to the home key for a final entry and coda.

Bach's two volumes of *The Well-Tempered Clavier*, twenty-four preludes and fugues in each of the major and minor keys, are widely regarded as the definitive demonstration of what the form can do. His unfinished *Art of Fugue* takes the project further, exploring how a single subject can be developed through every contrapuntal device -- inversion, retrograde, augmentation, diminution, mirror, double and triple fugue -- across an entire work.

## Invertible counterpoint

A piece of counterpoint is said to be invertible if its voices can be exchanged -- the upper line moved below the lower, or vice versa -- and the result remains musically and harmonically coherent. Double counterpoint at the octave is the most common case. Invertibility is a sought-after compositional skill because it allows the same material to be reused in multiple positions throughout a piece without sounding repetitive. Bach's three-part inventions and many of his fugues feature carefully constructed invertible passages.

## Why it matters today

Contrapuntal thinking did not die with the baroque. It continued through the classical period in the development sections of Mozart's symphonies, exploded in Beethoven's late quartets, and remained a pedagogical foundation for every subsequent generation. Modern composers from Hindemith to Stravinsky to John Adams have reached for fugal techniques when they want a particular kind of structural rigour. A working understanding of how independent lines combine -- whether you are writing for orchestra, for a string quartet, or for a synthesiser patch -- still begins with the lessons codified three centuries ago in Fux's treatise.
