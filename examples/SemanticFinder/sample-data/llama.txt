The llama is a domesticated species native to the Andes mountains of Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, and Chile. Standing about 1.8 metres tall at the head, it has been bred for over four thousand years as a beast of burden by Andean peoples. A healthy adult can carry roughly a quarter of its body weight over rough mountain terrain for many hours at a stretch, which made it indispensable to the Inca empire's road network. Llamas are intelligent, social herd animals, communicating with hums and ear positions, and famously expressing displeasure by spitting partially digested stomach contents at the offender. Their wool is coarser than that of their close relatives but is still spun into ropes, rugs, and outer garments. Today they are also kept in North America and Europe as guard animals for sheep flocks because of their alertness and willingness to chase off coyotes.
