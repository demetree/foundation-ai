The vicuña is the wild ancestor of the alpaca and the smallest member of its family, found above 3,500 metres on the windswept grasslands of the central Andes. Its fleece is the finest natural animal fibre in the world, with individual fibres measuring around 12 micrometres in diameter -- thinner than cashmere. Each animal yields only about 200 grams of usable fibre when shorn, and shearing is permitted just once every two or three years under tightly regulated rotating round-ups called chaccu, a tradition unchanged since Inca times. A finished vicuña overcoat can sell for the price of a small car. Hunted to near extinction by the 1960s, the species was saved by a coordinated international ban on trade in its wool and a community-based conservation programme that gave Andean villages exclusive rights to the regulated harvest. The population has since recovered to several hundred thousand animals.
