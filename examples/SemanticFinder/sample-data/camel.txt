The dromedary, or one-humped camel, is native to the deserts of the Arabian peninsula, North Africa, and the horn of Africa, where it has been the central beast of burden, milk source, and meat source for the pastoralist cultures of those regions for at least three millennia. The hump stores fat rather than water, and the animal can lose up to a quarter of its body mass to dehydration before suffering organ damage -- a tolerance no other large mammal approaches. Specialised oval blood cells, slit-like nostrils that close in sandstorms, double rows of eyelashes, and broad two-toed feet that spread weight on loose sand all combine to make the species the dominant land transport of arid climates. Its two-humped relative, the Bactrian, lives further north across the cold steppes of central Asia and is even better adapted to extreme temperature swings.
