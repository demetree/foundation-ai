The alpaca is a smaller cousin of the llama, also bred in the high altiplano of Peru and Bolivia, but raised primarily for its remarkably soft fleece rather than as a working animal. There are two breeds: the huacaya, with crimped fluffy fibre that gives them a teddy-bear silhouette, and the suri, whose fibre hangs in long silky locks. The fleece comes in twenty-two recognised natural colours from white through fawn and brown to true black, which means much commercial yarn never needs dyeing. Alpacas live in herds and are calm in temperament, rarely spitting at humans although they will at one another to settle disputes over food. They were first domesticated about six thousand years ago and were so valued by pre-Columbian cultures that fine fleece was reserved for nobility. Modern fibre is prized for being warmer than sheep's wool, lighter, and almost free of lanolin, making it hypoallergenic.
