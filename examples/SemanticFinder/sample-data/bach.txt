Johann Sebastian Bach was a German composer of the late baroque period whose work represents the high-water mark of contrapuntal writing in Western music. Born in Eisenach in 1685 into a family of professional musicians stretching back several generations, he held a series of court and church positions across Saxony and Thuringia, ending his career as cantor of the Thomasschule in Leipzig. His output is encyclopedic: more than two hundred sacred cantatas, the great Passions according to St Matthew and St John, the B-minor Mass, the Brandenburg Concertos, the Goldberg Variations, two volumes of preludes and fugues in every key called the Well-Tempered Clavier, and the unfinished Art of Fugue that explored the formal possibilities of a single subject across an entire work. Largely forgotten in the decades after his death in 1750, his music was revived in the nineteenth century by a generation of Romantic composers led by Felix Mendelssohn, and now sits at the centre of the Western art-music canon.
