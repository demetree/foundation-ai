Ludwig van Beethoven was a German composer and pianist whose work bridges the classical and romantic eras of Western music. Born in Bonn in 1770 and trained partly through study of the published works of Haydn and Mozart, he settled in Vienna in his early twenties and quickly established himself as the leading virtuoso pianist of the city before turning his ambition fully toward composition. His nine symphonies expand the form well beyond the proportions inherited from his predecessors and culminate in the choral finale of the Ninth, with its setting of Schiller's Ode to Joy. He also produced thirty-two piano sonatas, sixteen string quartets, the only opera Fidelio, and a Missa Solemnis. The progressive deafness that began in his late twenties did not slow his creative output but did force him to retreat from public performance, and his final period -- the late quartets in particular -- pushed harmonic and structural boundaries in ways that remained influential a century later.
